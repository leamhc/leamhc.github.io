Lea led the launch of a novel special topic series at Hong Kong’s largest and oldest academic public opinion institute. She was responsible for designing the survey, conducting research, comparing data, and visualizing findings. The study explored relationship statuses across demographic groups, methods by which couples meet, and their willingness to start a family, aiming to support evidence-based policymaking amid growing efforts by East Asian policymakers to introduce substantial baby bonuses and subsidies to combat the region’s population crisis.
<br>
<br>
As Hong Kong's fertility rate fell below South Korea's in 2022, reaching the lowest in the world, a poll conducted by the Hong Kong Public Opinion Research Institute revealed that over 60% of respondents expressed unwillingness to have children. The primary reasons cited were the “education system,” “political environment,” and “limited living space in the city.” In contrast, less than a quarter of respondents highlighted “career development” and “personal preferences regarding family” as their main considerations.

<br>
The study revealed that Hongkongers with less relationship experience are significantly less inclined to have children. Notably, 17% of Hong Kong adults identified as “A0” — a local slang term indicating that they have never been in a relationship (Available, Zero experience). This group includes 15% of women and nearly 20% of men. Among those identified as "A0," the proportion who still express a desire to have children is very low, comprising only 2% of this group.
<br>
<br>
The result highlighted generational differences in how Hongkongers meet their partners. Those born in the ‘60s primarily connected with their partners through work, while those born in the ‘70s and ‘80s mainly met through introductions by others. In contrast, the youngest generations — those born in the ‘90s and ‘00s — are more likely to meet their partners through school or dating apps.
<br>
<br>
A survey conducted by Stanford University found that the percentage of Americans meeting their partners through dating apps surged from 2% in 1995 to 39% in 2017, making it the most common way for Americans to find a partner, and a much higher rate compared to young people in Hong Kong. While the studies from the two regions are not directly comparable, these findings suggest that Hongkongers continue to rely on traditional methods such as school, work, or introductions by others to meet their partners.
